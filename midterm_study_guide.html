Biomedical Model & Biopsychosocial Model
▼
Core idea: Dominated 20th-century medicine. Explains disease through cellular/biochemical mechanisms. Patient = "mechanism in need of repair." Disease = deviation from measurable biological norm.

Key Features of the Biomedical Model

  • Reductionist — breaks disease into smallest biological component
  • Dualistic — separates mind from body
  • Excludes psychological, social, and behavioral dimensions
  • Patient is the site of disease, not a whole person
  • Does not account for why some people get sick and others don't

Cassell's Critique — "The Concept of Person"

Eric Cassell argued biomedicine cannot adequately address human suffering. He emphasized:
  • Person-centered focus — patients are more than their biology
  • Centrality of the individual — illness has personal meaning
  • Limits of biomedicine — cannot explain all clinical phenomena
  • Critique of dogma — challenges unquestioned assumptions

Biopsychosocial Model (Engel, 1977)

George Engel proposed the biopsychosocial model as a replacement for the biomedical model, incorporating three dimensions:

🧬 Biological

  • Genetics
  • Physiology
  • Pathogens
  • Cellular processes

🧠 Psychological

  • Cognition
  • Emotions
  • Behavior
  • Mental health

🤝 Social

  • Culture
  • Family
  • Socioeconomic status
  • Community

⚡ Key Shift

  • From disease → health
  • From parts → whole person
  • From reductionism → systems thinking
⚠️ Exam Tip
Be able to contrast biomedical vs. biopsychosocial — the biomedical model is reductionist and dualistic; biopsychosocial is holistic and integrative.

Health Belief Model (HBM)
▼
Origins: Developed in the 1950s by US Public Health Service researchers (Hochbaum, Rosenstock, Kegels & Leventhal) to explain why people wouldn't get free TB screenings.
Core premise: Health behavior is determined by personal beliefs about disease and strategies to reduce its occurrence.

The 6 Constructs

1. Perceived Susceptibility

  • "Am I at risk?"
  • Belief about chances of getting a condition
  • Can range from denial to high risk awareness

2. Perceived Severity

  • "How bad would it be?"
  • Medical consequences (death, disability)
  • Social consequences (job loss, relationships)

3. Perceived Benefits

  • "Will this action help?"
  • Belief that taking action reduces risk or severity
  • Must be believable to motivate action

4. Perceived Barriers

  • "What's in the way?"
  • Physical, psychological, financial obstacles
  • Most powerful predictor of behavior!

5. Cues to Action

  • Triggers that prompt behavior
  • Internal: symptoms, feeling ill
  • External: media, doctor advice, family illness

6. Self-Efficacy (added 1988)

  • Confidence in ability to take action
  • Added by Rosenstock, Strecher & Becker
  • Especially for complex behaviors
Perceived Threat (Susceptibility + Severity) + Benefits − Barriers + Cues to Action + Self-Efficacy → Health Behavior

Perceived Threat

Susceptibility × Severity = Perceived Threat. This is the motivating force. Without feeling threatened, there's no reason to change.

⚠️ Exam Tip
Perceived Barriers is consistently the strongest predictor of health behavior in the HBM. Know all 6 constructs and be able to give examples of each!
3
Self-Efficacy (Bandura, 1977)
▼
Definition: A person's belief in their own ability to perform a specific behavior to produce a desired outcome. Self-efficacy is behavior-specific — not general confidence.
Key distinction: Self-efficacy ≠ Outcome Expectancy. Self-efficacy = "Can I do it?" | Outcome expectancy = "Will it work?"

4 Sources of Self-Efficacy

⭐ 1. Mastery Experience (most powerful)

  • Personal success builds efficacy
  • Past performance is the strongest source
  • Failures can lower self-efficacy

👁️ 2. Vicarious Experience

  • Observing others succeed ("if they can, I can")
  • Role models especially powerful when similar to observer
  • Social modeling

💬 3. Verbal Persuasion

  • Encouragement from others
  • Coach, doctor, family saying "you can do it"
  • Weaker than mastery or vicarious

💓 4. Somatic & Emotional States

  • Physiological cues (heart racing, sweating)
  • Mood and emotional state affect confidence
  • Anxiety can reduce self-efficacy
⚠️ Exam Tip
Mastery Experience > Vicarious > Verbal Persuasion > Somatic. Know this order!

Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) & Integrated Behavioral Model (IBM)
▼
TPB (Ajzen, 1991) — an extension of the Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA). Adds Perceived Behavioral Control to account for behaviors not fully under volitional control.

Three Determinants of Intention → Behavior

1. Attitude Toward Behavior

  • Positive or negative evaluation of performing the behavior
  • = Behavioral beliefs × Outcome evaluations
  • "Is this a good/bad thing to do?"

2. Subjective Norm

  • Perceived social pressure
  • = Normative beliefs × Motivation to comply
  • "What would important people think?"

3. Perceived Behavioral Control (PBC)

  • Perceived ease/difficulty of performing behavior
  • = Control beliefs × Power of control factors
  • Similar to self-efficacy

→ Behavioral Intention

  • Strongest predictor of actual behavior
  • Attitude + Subjective Norm + PBC → Intention
  • Intention → Behavior (when control is present)
Attitude + Subjective Norm + Perceived Behavioral Control → Intention → Behavior

Integrated Behavioral Model (IBM)

IBM extends TPB by adding variables that bridge the intention-behavior gap. Even when people intend to act, they may not follow through.

IBM adds to TPB:

Personal Agency Knowledge & Skills Salience of Behavior Environmental Constraints Habit / Automaticity
FeatureTPBIBM
Core constructsAttitude, Subj. Norm, PBCSame + more
Behavioral intentionCentralCentral

⚠️ Exam Tip
IBM was developed specifically for health promotion. Know what IBM adds that TPB doesn't have — knowledge/skills, environmental constraints, salience, and habit are the key additions.

Transtheoretical Model (TTM) / Stages of Change
▼
Developed by: Prochaska & DiClemente (1983). Originally developed from studying smoking cessation. TTM integrates multiple theories — hence "transtheoretical."

The 5 Stages of Change

1. Precontemplation

  • Not thinking about change
  • No intention to act in next 6 months
  • "I don't have a problem"
  • May be in denial or uninformed

2. Contemplation

  • Thinking about change
  • Plans to act within next 6 months
  • Ambivalent — sees pros AND cons
  • "Chronic contemplators" may stay here

3. Preparation

  • Ready to act within next 30 days
  • Taking small steps toward change
  • Has a plan in place
  • "Action threshold" reached

4. Action

  • Changed behavior < 6 months ago
  • Most visible, effortful stage
  • High risk of relapse
  • Requires commitment and energy

5. Maintenance

  • Sustained behavior change > 6 months
  • Preventing relapse is primary task
  • Less temptation over time
  • May reach "termination" stage

6. Termination (optional)

  • Zero temptation, 100% confidence
  • Not always included
  • Most people cycle through stages
  • Relapse is normal, not failure

Decisional Balance

Pros of Changing vs. Cons of Changing → Movement Through Stages

In early stages (Precontemplation), cons outweigh pros. As people move forward, pros increase relative to cons.

Processes of Change (10 total)

Consciousness Raising Dramatic Relief Environmental Reevaluation Social Liberation Self-Reevaluation Self-Liberation Helping Relationships Counter Conditioning Reinforcement Management Stimulus Control

First 5 = Experiential (earlier stages). Last 5 = Behavioral (later stages).

⚠️ Exam Tip
Know the time markers: Precontemplation/Contemplation = no action in next 6 months; Preparation = action in next 30 days; Action = changed <6 months; Maintenance = changed >6 months.

Social Ecological Framework (SEM)
▼
Core idea: Health behaviors are influenced by multiple levels of the social and physical environment — not just individual choices. Based on Bronfenbrenner's ecological systems theory.

Levels of Influence

1. Individual (Intrapersonal)

  • Knowledge, attitudes, beliefs
  • Self-efficacy
  • Demographics, skills
  • Personal history

2. Interpersonal

  • Family, friends, peers
  • Social networks, social support
  • Social norms
  • Cultural expectations

3. Organizational / Community

  • Schools, workplaces, churches
  • Formal and informal rules
  • Access to resources
  • Neighborhood environment

4. Public Policy / Societal

  • Local, state, federal laws
  • Policy and regulations
  • Systemic inequities
  • Social determinants of health
💡 Key Insight
SEM recognizes that you can't address health behavior ONLY at the individual level. Change at multiple levels = more sustainable impact. Upstream (policy) changes affect the most people.

Health Impact Pyramid (Frieden, 2010)
▼
Developed by: Thomas Frieden (CDC Director). Shows the relationship between population impact and type of intervention.
Counseling & Education
Clinical Interventions
Long-lasting Protective Interventions
Changing the Context (Default Options)
Socioeconomic Factors
← More population impact | More individual effort required →

Each Level Explained

Level (Bottom→Top)ExampleImpact
SocioeconomicEducation, poverty reduction, stable housingGreatest — addresses root causes
Changing ContextCalorie labels, safe neighborhoods, bike lanesMakes healthy choice the default/easy choice
Long-lasting InterventionsVaccines, fluoridation, surgical interventionsOne-time action, sustained effect
Clinical InterventionsMedications, screenings, ongoing treatmentRequires ongoing access and adherence
Counseling & EducationSmoking cessation counseling, nutrition edLeast impact on population; high individual effort
⚠️ Exam Tip
Bottom of pyramid = biggest population impact, least individual effort required. Top = most individual effort required, smallest population impact. This is counterintuitive — lower levels are MORE effective at the population level!

Historical Timeline of Health Theory
▼
1948
WHO definition of health: "A state of complete physical, mental, and social wellbeing, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity"
1950s
Health Belief Model developed by Hochbaum, Rosenstock, Kegels & Leventhal at US Public Health Service. Originally to explain TB screening non-compliance.
1977
Biopsychosocial Model proposed by George Engel as critique of the biomedical model. Self-Efficacy theory published by Bandura.
1983
Transtheoretical Model (TTM) developed by Prochaska & DiClemente based on smoking cessation research.
1988
Self-Efficacy added to HBM by Rosenstock, Strecher & Becker as the 6th construct.
1991
Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) formalized by Icek Ajzen, extending Theory of Reasoned Action with Perceived Behavioral Control.
2010
Health Impact Pyramid published by Thomas Frieden (CDC Director), framing population health interventions by impact level.
